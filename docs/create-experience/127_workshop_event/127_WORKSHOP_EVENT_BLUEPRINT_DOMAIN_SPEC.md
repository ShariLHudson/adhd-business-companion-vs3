# 127A — Workshop Event Blueprint Domain Specification

## Purpose

This file defines the domain intelligence that must be represented in the Workshop Event Blueprint.

It is implementation guidance and certification reference data for Cursor.

It must not become a second runtime or duplicate Blueprint definition.

## Workshop success model

A workshop succeeds when participants actively learn, practice, decide, create, or apply something meaningful.

Attendance and satisfaction alone are not sufficient.

The Blueprint should evaluate:

- clarity of outcomes
- relevance to participants
- balance of input and participation
- quality of activities
- usefulness of outputs
- accessibility
- energy and pacing
- implementation after the workshop
- evidence of understanding or progress

## Workshop patterns

Support adaptive planning for:

### Short workshop

Best when:

- one focused outcome is enough
- audience already has context
- time is limited
- a practical first step is the goal

Primary risks:

- trying to teach too much
- skipping practice
- rushing questions
- weak follow-up

### Half-day workshop

Best when:

- two to four outcomes are realistic
- participants need learning plus practice
- a meaningful output can be completed

Primary risks:

- insufficient breaks
- overpacked agenda
- weak transition from learning to application

### Full-day workshop

Best when:

- depth, practice, and planning are required
- participants need time for discussion and application

Primary risks:

- fatigue
- too much content
- poor pacing
- weak afternoon energy
- inadequate lunch and recovery time

### Multi-session workshop

Best when:

- learning needs time between sessions
- participants must apply or practice
- accountability matters

Primary risks:

- attendance drop-off
- unclear between-session work
- weak continuity
- fragmented materials

### Virtual workshop

Best when:

- geography is broad
- participants can access technology
- content and activities can work online

Primary risks:

- fatigue
- technical barriers
- low participation
- poor breakout design
- unclear digital materials

### Hybrid workshop

Best when:

- both in-person and remote access are essential
- production support is available

Primary risks:

- remote participants becoming secondary
- audio problems
- facilitator overload
- unequal participation

### Working session

Best when:

- participants need to produce a plan, decision, framework, or artifact
- less instruction and more facilitation are appropriate

Primary risks:

- unclear deliverable
- insufficient preparation
- no decision rules
- lack of ownership after the session

## Learning outcome quality

Good outcomes should describe observable change.

Strong outcome patterns:

- Participants can explain...
- Participants can identify...
- Participants can compare...
- Participants can create...
- Participants can practice...
- Participants can decide...
- Participants can complete...
- Participants can apply...
- Participants leave with...

Weak outcome patterns:

- Participants will understand everything about...
- Participants will be inspired...
- Participants will learn a lot about...
- Participants will become experts in...

The Blueprint should help convert vague outcomes into usable outcomes.

## Content selection rule

Every content item should answer at least one question:

- Is this essential to an outcome?
- Is this required for an activity?
- Is this needed for a decision?
- Is this needed for safe or correct application?
- Is this better as a follow-up resource?

If not, remove or defer it.

## Activity quality

Every activity should define:

- purpose
- connection to an outcome
- instructions
- time
- grouping
- materials
- expected output
- facilitator behavior
- debrief
- alternative participation
- virtual adaptation
- contingency

Activities should not exist merely to make the workshop feel interactive.

## Debrief quality

A useful debrief may ask:

- What did you notice?
- What worked?
- What was difficult?
- What changed in your thinking?
- What does this mean for your situation?
- What will you do differently?
- What do you still need?

Activities without debrief often produce less learning than intended.

## Participant journey standards

### Before the workshop

Participants should know:

- why they are attending
- what they will gain
- what to prepare
- what materials to bring
- what the format will feel like
- how participation will work
- what accessibility support exists

### At the beginning

Participants should receive:

- a clear welcome
- outcomes
- agenda
- participation expectations
- break information
- technology or room guidance
- psychological safety guidance
- permission to ask questions or request help

### During

Participants should experience:

- manageable content blocks
- meaningful activity
- clear instructions
- examples
- practice
- reflection
- breaks
- support
- predictable transitions
- time to ask questions

### At the end

Participants should leave with:

- a summary
- completed output or action plan
- next steps
- resources
- follow-up expectations
- a way to ask questions
- confidence about what to do next

## Facilitation standards

Facilitators should:

- make expectations clear
- protect time
- adapt without losing outcomes
- manage dominant participants
- invite quieter participants without pressure
- avoid humiliating or forced participation
- explain activities clearly
- debrief meaningfully
- recognize confusion
- respond respectfully
- maintain boundaries
- preserve psychological safety
- close intentionally

## Accessibility and neurodiversity standards

The Blueprint should encourage:

- predictable agenda
- written instructions
- readable materials
- visible timing
- captions
- reduced visual clutter
- movement choices
- seated options
- quiet space
- regular breaks
- no forced public speaking
- no forced disclosure
- alternatives to role play
- clear transition warnings
- time to process
- digital copies
- sensory-aware room design
- support for different learning preferences

## Materials standards

Participant materials should be:

- directly connected to outcomes
- easy to navigate
- readable
- usable during and after the workshop
- consistent with the agenda
- not overloaded with decorative content
- available in accessible formats
- clearly versioned

Facilitator materials should make delivery easier, not duplicate the participant workbook.

## Energy and pacing guidance

Suggested planning considerations:

- shorter content blocks
- practice after key concepts
- breaks before attention collapses
- movement or reset after demanding work
- lower-energy activity after lunch
- clear transitions
- extra time for complex instructions
- buffer for questions
- buffer for technology issues
- realistic closing time

## Evaluation model

Evaluation should align to outcomes.

Possible levels:

### Reaction

- Was this useful?
- Was the experience accessible?
- Was the pacing appropriate?

### Learning

- What did participants understand?
- What skill or decision improved?

### Application

- What will participants do?
- Did they complete the intended output?

### Follow-through

- What happened after the workshop?
- Was the learning used?

Do not require all levels for every workshop.

## Follow-up quality

Follow-up may include:

- thank-you
- materials
- action plan
- recording
- implementation reminder
- office hours
- accountability
- coaching
- peer check-in
- additional resources
- next workshop
- feedback summary
- promised answers

## Evidence and learning

After the workshop, capture:

- outcomes achieved
- participant outputs
- questions that remained
- activities that worked
- content that was unnecessary
- timing issues
- accessibility successes or failures
- participant implementation
- facilitator learning
- reusable improvements
- wins
- accomplishments
- evidence for future decisions

## Domain certification questions

Before production certification, verify:

1. Does the Blueprint define real outcomes?
2. Does it prevent content overload?
3. Does it include meaningful practice?
4. Does every activity have a purpose and debrief?
5. Does it support different experience levels?
6. Does it support in-person, virtual, and hybrid delivery?
7. Does it protect accessibility and psychological safety?
8. Does it manage energy and pacing?
9. Does it support implementation after the workshop?
10. Does it define success beyond satisfaction?
11. Does it remain simple enough in Quick Start?
12. Does it surface commonly forgotten facilitation and production details?
