# 126A — Networking Event Blueprint Domain Specification

## Purpose

This file defines the domain intelligence that must be represented in the Networking Event Blueprint.

It is implementation guidance and certification reference data for Cursor.

It must not become a second runtime or a duplicate of the registered Blueprint definition.

## Networking event success model

A networking event succeeds when it helps the right people make useful, comfortable, respectful, and actionable connections.

Attendance alone is not success.

The Blueprint should evaluate:

- relevance of attendee mix
- ease of entering conversations
- quality of introductions
- inclusion and accessibility
- usefulness of the event structure
- follow-up activity
- relationship continuation
- host and partner outcomes

## Event patterns

Support adaptive planning for:

### Open mixer

Best when:

- attendees already share a clear context
- event is short
- host capacity is limited
- informal connection is appropriate

Primary risks:

- cliques
- isolated attendees
- shallow conversations
- unclear value

### Facilitated networking

Best when:

- attendees need help connecting
- audience is mixed
- inclusion matters
- host wants intentional outcomes

Primary risks:

- over-structuring
- awkward prompts
- insufficient transition time

### Speed networking

Best when:

- many introductions are desired
- timing can be controlled
- attendees understand the format

Primary risks:

- sensory overload
- rushed conversations
- accessibility barriers
- shallow matching

### Table-based networking

Best when:

- discussion themes matter
- people benefit from seated conversation
- food or learning is included

Primary risks:

- weak table composition
- one person dominating
- limited cross-table movement

### Matched networking

Best when:

- participant information is available
- the event has defined relationship goals
- hosts can support matching

Primary risks:

- poor matches
- privacy concerns
- overpromising outcomes

### Networking plus content

Best when:

- a speaker, panel, or workshop creates shared context
- content naturally leads to connection

Primary risks:

- too little networking time
- content dominates
- audience leaves before connections form

### Recurring networking series

Best when:

- community development is the goal
- relationships need repeated contact
- hosts can sustain consistency

Primary risks:

- declining novelty
- unclear progression
- weak member retention
- repetitive experience

## Attendee journey quality checks

### Before the event

The attendee should know:

- who the event is for
- why it may be useful
- what the format will feel like
- whether they need to prepare
- what accessibility support exists
- what to wear when relevant
- where to go
- what to expect on arrival
- whether contact information may be shared

### On arrival

The attendee should be able to:

- find check-in
- understand what happens next
- receive readable identification
- enter the room without confusion
- find help
- avoid standing alone without support
- understand participation choices

### During the event

The attendee should have:

- a clear welcome
- usable conversation support
- sufficient time
- comfortable transitions
- opt-out choices
- access to refreshments and rest
- support if they become overwhelmed
- fair opportunity to participate

### At closing

The attendee should know:

- what happens next
- how to continue connections
- how to give feedback
- whether another event is planned
- what follow-up will arrive

## Host behavior standards

Hosts and facilitators should:

- notice isolated attendees
- avoid spending the event only with familiar people
- make relevant introductions
- explain activities clearly
- model respectful conversation
- manage time
- protect inclusion
- respond to discomfort
- close the event intentionally
- follow through on promised introductions

## Conversation prompt quality

Good prompts should:

- be easy to understand
- not require disclosure of private information
- be relevant to the event purpose
- work for different experience levels
- invite useful conversation
- avoid embarrassment
- allow short or deeper answers
- not assume everyone owns a business
- not force public speaking

Examples:

- What are you working on right now?
- What kind of connection would be useful today?
- What is one challenge you would enjoy comparing notes about?
- What is something you know that could help someone else here?
- What kind of person would you like to meet?
- What is one idea you are exploring?

## Matching criteria

When matching is used, possible criteria include:

- goals
- expertise
- needs
- industry
- role
- location
- stage of business
- collaboration interest
- referral potential
- mentoring interest
- complementary resources

Matching must avoid sensitive profiling and must respect user consent.

## Accessibility and neurodiversity standards

The Blueprint should encourage:

- predictable event flow
- clear written instructions
- readable badges
- optional structured prompts
- quiet seating
- no forced introductions
- no forced touching
- no surprise public speaking
- flexible participation
- visible timing
- transition warnings
- captions for virtual content
- lower-sensory options
- easy access to exits and restrooms
- simple ways to request help

## Event-day host kit

The complete plan may generate:

- contact list
- setup checklist
- room map
- event timeline
- host script
- facilitator prompts
- attendee-support notes
- sponsor commitments
- vendor contacts
- accessibility requests
- emergency information
- equipment list
- closing script
- follow-up commitments

## Follow-up quality

Follow-up should distinguish:

- general thank-you
- promised introductions
- resource delivery
- sponsor follow-up
- attendee feedback
- no-show communication
- next-event invitation
- community invitation
- relationship opportunities
- internal debrief tasks

## Evidence and learning

After the event, capture:

- meaningful introductions
- partnerships begun
- referrals generated
- attendee comments
- accessibility successes or failures
- host observations
- follow-up actions
- repeat-event interest
- reusable improvements
- wins
- accomplishments
- evidence for future decisions

## Domain certification questions

Before production certification, verify:

1. Does the Blueprint design connections rather than merely attendance?
2. Does it support people arriving alone?
3. Does it address social anxiety and neurodiversity?
4. Does it prevent forced participation?
5. Does it include host and facilitator responsibilities?
6. Does it support multiple networking formats?
7. Does it adapt to attendance size and event purpose?
8. Does it include follow-up and relationship continuation?
9. Does it define success beyond attendance?
10. Does it surface commonly forgotten operational details?
11. Does it preserve privacy and consent?
12. Does it remain simple enough in Quick Start?
