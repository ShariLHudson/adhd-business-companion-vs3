# Purpose
Many meaningful activities require more than knowing what needs to happen.
They require understanding:
- what comes first
- what comes next

- when adjustments are needed

**"How does a person determine the order required to move from intention toward completion?"**

# Sequencing Defined
Sequencing is the executive function process of organizing actions, information, or events into a meaningful order.
It supports the ability to:
- understand relationships between steps
- identify dependencies
- determine starting points
- recognize progression
- adjust order when circumstances change
Sequencing transforms separate actions into coordinated movement.
---
# Guiding Principles
## Order Creates Clarity
Knowing the relationship between actions reduces uncertainty.
A clear sequence helps transform complexity into manageable progress.
---
## Sequencing Is Different From Planning
Planning considers the future structure of action.
Sequencing focuses specifically on the order and relationship of components.
They work together but remain separate concepts.
---
## Sequencing Depends on Context
The best sequence depends on:
- desired outcome
- available resources
- timing
- dependencies
- changing conditions
There is rarely one universal sequence.
---
## Effective Sequencing Reduces Cognitive Demand
A meaningful order reduces the need to constantly determine what comes next.
Structure supports action.
---
## Sequences Can Change
A sequence that works in one situation may require adjustment in another.
Adaptation is part of effective sequencing.
---
## Simple Sequences Support Action
Overly complicated sequences can create additional executive demands.
Effective sequences balance completeness with usability.
---

# Sequencing and Other Executive Domains
Sequencing interacts with multiple executive processes.
