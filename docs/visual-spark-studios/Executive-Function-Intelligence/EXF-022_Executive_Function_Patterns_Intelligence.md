Defines how Executive Function Intelligence recognizes, organizes, and uses recurring executive patterns to create more personalized support over time.

# Purpose
Executive functioning is not experienced the same way every day.
People often develop recurring patterns around:
- when they function best
- when demands become difficult
- what environments support success
- what situations increase friction
- what strategies improve performance
Executive Function Patterns Intelligence exists to understand these recurring relationships.
It answers the question:
**"What recurring executive patterns help explain how this person functions?"**

# Executive Function Patterns Defined
Executive Function Patterns are recurring relationships between executive functioning and surrounding conditions.
Patterns may include:
- situations where executive functioning improves
- situations where executive functioning becomes more difficult
- conditions that support success
- conditions that increase cognitive demand
Patterns are observations.
They are not permanent labels.
---
# Guiding Principles
## Patterns Inform, They Do Not Define
A pattern describes repeated experience.
It does not determine what a person can or cannot do.
---
## Patterns Require Time
A single event does not create a reliable pattern.
Patterns emerge through repeated observation.
---
## Patterns Are Contextual
Executive patterns may depend upon:
- environment
- workload
- emotional state
- physical condition
- familiarity
- support systems
---
## Patterns Can Change
A strategy that works today may need adjustment later.
Executive Function Intelligence expects growth and change.
---
## Patterns Should Increase Understanding
The purpose of identifying patterns is not prediction alone.
The purpose is creating better support.
---
# Executive Function Pattern Categories
Executive Function Intelligence may recognize several categories.
---
# Capacity Patterns
Patterns related to available executive resources.
Examples:
- times of higher mental energy
- periods of reduced capacity
- recovery needs
- demand tolerance
---
# Initiation Patterns
Patterns related to beginning meaningful action.
Examples:
- environments that support starting
- conditions that increase hesitation
- types of tasks that require additional support
---
# Attention Patterns
Patterns related to directing and maintaining attention.
Examples:
- preferred work environments
- interruption sensitivity
- focus conditions
---
# Planning Patterns
Patterns related to preparing future action.
Examples:
- planning approaches that work
- situations where planning becomes difficult
---
# Organization Patterns
Patterns related to maintaining useful structure.
Examples:
- systems that remain sustainable
- organizational approaches that create friction
---
# Recovery Patterns
Patterns related to restoring executive resources.
Examples:
- activities that support recovery
- conditions that increase depletion
---
# Pattern Interpretation Process
## Observe
Identify repeated experiences.
---
## Compare
Look for similarities and differences.
---
## Understand Context
Determine surrounding conditions.
---
## Validate
Avoid assuming a pattern without sufficient evidence.
---
## Apply Carefully
Use patterns to personalize support.
Do not use patterns to limit possibilities.
