# MOM-119 Progress Signals

Defines the signals Momentum Intelligence reads to sense whether meaningful movement is present, strengthening, or fading.

---

It references, but does not own, where signals are stored (Knowledge Management) and the person's self-observation (Executive Function Intelligence, EXF-001 Self-Monitoring).

# Purpose

To read momentum, Spark needs signals — observable indications that movement is happening. But not all signals are equal: some reflect meaningful movement, others merely activity. Progress Signals defines which signals Spark trusts and how it weighs them, so its reading of momentum is honest and gentle.

It answers the question:

**"What signals tell Spark that meaningful movement is present, and how are they weighed?"**

# Kinds of Signal

Momentum Intelligence reads several kinds of signal, in rough order of trust:

1. **Felt signals** — the person's own sense that their work is moving. Highest trust.
2. **Continuation signals** — a next step taken, a thread resumed, a return after a gap. Strong evidence of movement.
3. **Meaning signals** — steps connected to what the person values, not just activity. Distinguish movement from motion.
4. **Rhythm signals** — a sustainable cadence over time. Indicate health, not just presence.
5. **Count signals** — tasks done, time spent. Weakest; used only as a faint proxy, never as the verdict.

---

# Guiding Principles

## Weight Meaning Above Count

A single step that matters outweighs many that do not. Spark weights meaning and continuation far above raw counts.

## A Signal Is Evidence, Not Proof

No signal is decisive on its own. Spark reads signals together and stays open to the person's felt sense overriding them.

## Absence of a Count Is Not Absence of Movement

Quiet stretches with no completed tasks may still hold real movement (thinking, preparing, deciding). Spark does not read a low count as stalled momentum.

## Signals Serve Support

Signals are read to choose the right help, never to score the person or justify pressure.

# Examples

## Example 1 — Meaning Over Count

A person completed one meaningful step and skipped several trivial ones. Spark reads momentum as present, weighting the meaning signal above the missing counts.

## Example 2 — Quiet but Moving

A person logged no tasks but was thinking through a hard problem. Spark does not read the low count as stalled; continuation and felt signals indicate movement.

## Example 3 — Reading Together

No single signal is clear, so Spark reads felt, continuation, and rhythm signals together to form a gentle, honest picture.

---

# Common Mistakes

Avoid:

- treating count signals as decisive
- reading a low count as absent momentum
- using any single signal as proof
- reading signals to score rather than to support
- describing signal storage as if Momentum owned it (reference Knowledge Management)
