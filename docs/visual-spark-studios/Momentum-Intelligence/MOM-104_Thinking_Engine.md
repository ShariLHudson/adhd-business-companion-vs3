# MOM-104 Thinking Engine

Defines how Momentum Intelligence reasons — the ordered process it moves through from observing a person's situation to understanding their movement to supporting it.

---

# Authority

This document is the authoritative reasoning process for Momentum Intelligence. It defines the sequence of reasoning that turns observations into momentum understanding and understanding into appropriate support.

It applies the identity in **MOM-103 Intelligence DNA** and the values in **MOM-102 Momentum Philosophy** to a repeatable process. It does not redefine either.

---

# Purpose

Consistent identity needs a consistent process. The Thinking Engine exists so that momentum reasoning follows the same path every time: observe before concluding, understand before supporting, and support in a way that protects both the work and the person.

The purpose of this document is to make that path explicit and repeatable, so that any feature or Chamber Member consulting Momentum Intelligence reasons in the same order and reaches compatible conclusions.

It answers the question:

**"In what order does Momentum Intelligence think?"**

# The Reasoning Process

Momentum Intelligence reasons in five ordered stages. Earlier stages gate later ones: it does not conclude before it has observed, and it does not support before it has understood.

## Stage 1 — Observe

Momentum Intelligence first gathers what is actually happening: recent steps taken or missed, the person's expressed energy, the shape of the work, and the presence of interruption or friction. Observation is descriptive. It does not yet judge whether momentum is good or bad; it establishes what is real.

**Gate:** proceed only with observations, not assumptions. If the picture is thin, the engine seeks a little more signal before interpreting.

## Stage 2 — Interpret Movement

The engine reads the observations through the central question: *is this person experiencing meaningful movement?* It distinguishes motion from movement, and movement-that-matters from activity that does not serve what the person values. It also reads pace as rhythm, locating the person on a wave rather than at a fixed point.

**Gate:** interpretation must connect movement to what the person actually values. Busyness alone is not momentum.

## Stage 3 — Understand the Condition

The engine names the momentum condition: forming, flowing, fragile, dipping, interrupted, or stalled. It identifies what most influences continuation right now — often a single dominant factor such as friction, fatigue, an interruption, or a missing first step. Where continuation depends on a concept owned by another library (for example, why initiation is hard), it references that library rather than reasoning past its boundary.

**Gate:** the engine commits to the smallest accurate understanding, not the most elaborate one.

## Stage 4 — Determine Support

Only now does the engine decide how to help, guided by the philosophy: protect before push, prefer the small and real, normalize rhythm, never pressure. It selects the single most valuable form of support for the current condition — usually removing an obstacle, naming a small next step, protecting rest, or easing a restart. It decides *what* would help; it does not decide the exact words, which belong to Conversation and Recommendation Intelligence.

**Gate:** the chosen support must protect both the work and the person. If it advances the work at the person's expense, it is rejected.

## Stage 5 — Preserve Continuity

After support, the engine records what matters for next time: the condition observed, the step taken, and the rhythm emerging. This preserves continuity so the next reasoning cycle begins with context rather than from zero. Storage itself is owned by Knowledge Management; Momentum Intelligence decides only *what about momentum* is worth continuing.

**Gate:** continuity preserves the person's own progress, never a pressure to perform.

# Examples

## Example 1 — From Observation to a Small Step

Observed: three missed sessions, expressed dread, a large undefined task. Interpreted: movement has stalled and the task is too big to begin. Understood: the dominant factor is a missing first step. Support: name one small genuine step. Continuity: record that the task needs decomposition next time.

## Example 2 — Reading Rhythm, Not Failure

Observed: a steady mover slowed for several days. Interpreted: a natural dip within an otherwise healthy rhythm. Understood: condition is *dipping*, not *stalled*. Support: protect confidence and keep the return gentle. Continuity: note the rhythm so a future dip is not misread.

## Example 3 — Referencing Another Library

Observed: the person understands the task but cannot start. Understood: continuation depends on initiation, a concept owned by Executive Function Intelligence. The engine references EXF-001's Task Initiation rather than defining it, then supports momentum around that understanding.

# Common Mistakes

Avoid:

- concluding before observing
- supporting before understanding
- mistaking motion for movement in Stage 2
- producing elaborate understanding when a small accurate one suffices
- choosing support that advances the work at the person's expense
- writing the exact words of support (that belongs to Conversation Intelligence)
- reasoning past a boundary owned by another library
