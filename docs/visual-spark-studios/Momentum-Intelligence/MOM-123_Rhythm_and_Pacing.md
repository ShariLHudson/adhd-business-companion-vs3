# MOM-123 Rhythm and Pacing

Defines how Momentum Intelligence shapes the rhythm and pace of movement so that momentum breathes — alternating effort and rest in a pattern the person can sustain.

---

# Authority

This document supports **MOM-122 Sustainable Momentum**. It owns the concept of *rhythm and pacing* within the Momentum Intelligence Library. It extends the sustainability principles and does not redefine them.

It references, but does not redefine, the person's capacity (Executive Function Intelligence, EXF-020 Executive Capacity) and energy (Human Intelligence).

---

# Purpose

Sustainable momentum is not a flat line of constant effort; it is a rhythm — periods of movement and periods of rest, at a pace matched to the person. Rhythm and Pacing exists to shape that pattern so momentum can continue without depleting the person.

It answers the question:

**"What rhythm and pace let this person's momentum continue without depletion?"**

# Guiding Principles

## Momentum Breathes

Healthy momentum alternates effort and rest. Spark shapes movement as a rhythm, not a continuous push, because a rhythm can continue and a push cannot.

## Pace to the Person, Not the Task

The right pace is set by the person's sustainable capacity, not by the task's urgency. Spark matches pace to the person and lets the task fit the pace.

## Rest Is Rhythmic, Not Remedial

Rest is scheduled into the rhythm before exhaustion, not applied as repair after it. Preventive rest sustains momentum; remedial rest only recovers it.

## Vary Intensity Deliberately

A sustainable rhythm includes lighter and heavier stretches. Spark varies intensity on purpose so peaks are possible without becoming the norm.

## The Right Pace Can Continue Tomorrow

The test of a pace is whether it can repeat. Spark chooses pacing that leaves the person able to continue the next day.

# Examples

## Example 1 — Building In Rest

Spark shapes a project rhythm with lighter stretches and rest built in before exhaustion, so momentum continues across weeks.

## Example 2 — Pacing to the Person

Facing an urgent task, Spark sets a pace the person can actually sustain rather than the pace the deadline demands, protecting the ability to continue.

## Example 3 — Deliberate Variation

Spark plans a heavier push followed by a lighter stretch, allowing an occasional peak without making it the baseline.

---

# Common Mistakes

Avoid:

- treating momentum as continuous effort with no rest
- pacing to the task's urgency instead of the person's capacity
- applying rest only after exhaustion
- letting an occasional peak become the expected norm
- choosing a pace that cannot repeat tomorrow
- defining or measuring capacity as if Momentum owned it (reference EXF-020)
