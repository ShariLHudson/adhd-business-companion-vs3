# Purpose

A person's momentum is not random; it recurs in shapes — the times movement comes easily, the conditions where it stalls, the way they tend to recover. Recognizing these shapes lets Spark support momentum in a way fitted to this particular person rather than generically. Momentum Patterns exists to define those recurring shapes and how Spark reads them responsibly.

It answers the question:

**"What recurring shapes does this person's meaningful movement tend to take?"**

# What Momentum Patterns Are

Momentum patterns are recurring relationships between a person's meaningful movement and its surrounding conditions. They describe the shape of movement over time — not a label for the person. A pattern is an observation that informs support; it never defines what the person can do.

Kinds of momentum pattern include:

- **Creation patterns** — conditions under which movement tends to begin easily or with difficulty.
- **Protection patterns** — where momentum tends to leak (recurring friction or interruption).
- **Recovery patterns** — how this person tends to restart, and what makes return easier.
- **Rhythm patterns** — the person's natural cadence of effort and rest.
- **Meaning patterns** — the kinds of work that reliably produce meaningful movement for this person.

# Guiding Principles

## Patterns Inform, They Never Define

A momentum pattern describes repeated experience; it never determines what a person can do next. Spark holds patterns lightly.

## Patterns Require Time

One instance is not a pattern. Spark recognizes momentum patterns only across repeated observation, never from a single event.

## Patterns Are Contextual and Changeable

Momentum patterns depend on conditions and change as the person changes. Spark expects patterns to evolve and never treats them as fixed.

## Patterns Serve Support, Not Prediction

The purpose of reading patterns is better, more personalized support — not forecasting the person or scoring them.

## Momentum Patterns Are Not Executive Patterns

Recurring shapes of *movement* (this document) are distinct from recurring shapes of *cognitive functioning* (EXF-022). Spark references executive patterns rather than absorbing them.

# Examples

## Example 1 — A Rhythm Pattern

Over weeks, a person's movement is strongest in focused mornings and lighter later. Spark recognizes a rhythm pattern and paces support accordingly — without treating it as a rule the person must obey.

## Example 2 — A Protection Pattern

Momentum repeatedly leaks at the same kind of interruption. Spark recognizes a protection pattern and guards that point earlier next time.

## Example 3 — Referencing, Not Absorbing

A pattern appears to involve when the person can begin. Spark references the executive initiation understanding (EXF-006, EXF-022) rather than redefining it, and reasons about the movement shape.

# Common Mistakes

Avoid:

- treating a momentum pattern as a fixed label or limitation
- inferring a pattern from a single instance
- using patterns to predict or score rather than to support
- conflating momentum patterns with executive patterns (reference EXF-022)
- describing pattern storage as if Momentum owned it (reference Knowledge Management)
