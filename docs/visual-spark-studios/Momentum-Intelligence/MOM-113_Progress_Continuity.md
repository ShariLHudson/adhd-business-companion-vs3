# MOM-113 Progress Continuity

Defines progress continuity — the thread that connects one stretch of movement to the next so that progress accumulates rather than resets. This is one of the concepts Momentum Intelligence authoritatively owns.

---

It extends the protection principles and inherits the Core. It references the wording of resumption (Conversation Intelligence) and the storage of continuity data (Knowledge Management) without owning either.

# Purpose

Progress that resets each time is not momentum; it is a series of unrelated starts. Continuity is what makes yesterday's movement count toward today's — the thread that lets a person pick up where they left off instead of beginning again.

The purpose of this document is to define continuity and how Spark preserves it, so that progress accumulates across sessions, days, and interruptions. It answers the question:

**"How does progress carry forward so it accumulates rather than resets?"**

# What Progress Continuity Is

Progress continuity is the preserved connection between a completed stretch of movement and the next one. It has three parts:

1. **The thread** — the next step, held so movement can resume without re-deciding.
2. **The context** — enough of where things stood that returning does not require rebuilding understanding.
3. **The felt progress** — the person's sense that what they did still counts, so returning feels like continuing, not restarting.

When all three are preserved, a gap in time does not become a gap in progress.

---

# Guiding Principles

## Preserve the Next Step Above All

The single most valuable thing to carry forward is the next step. If the person knows exactly where to resume, continuity largely survives.

## Carry Context, Not Clutter

Continuity preserves enough context to resume, not a complete record of everything. Too much carried forward becomes friction of its own.

## Returning Should Feel Like Continuing

Continuity protects the person's sense that their earlier movement still counts. Returning should feel like rejoining a thread, never like starting over.

## Continuity Is Not a Debt

Preserved progress is an invitation to continue, never a backlog that accuses. Spark presents continuity as a warm handoff, not an obligation.

## Distinguish a Gap From a Loss

A pause with continuity intact is a gap, not a loss. Spark treats a return after a gap as continuation (this document) and only escalates to recovery (MOM-114–017) when the thread itself was lost.

# Examples

## Example 1 — Resuming a Session

A person returns after a day away. Because the next step and minimal context were preserved, Spark helps them resume exactly where they left off, and it feels like continuing rather than restarting.

## Example 2 — Context Without Clutter

Spark carries forward just enough of where things stood to resume, without dumping a full history that would itself become friction.

## Example 3 — A Warm Handoff, Not a Debt

After a gap, Spark presents the preserved thread as an invitation to continue, never as an accusing backlog of what was left undone.

---

# Common Mistakes

Avoid:

- losing the next step across a gap
- carrying forward so much context that it becomes clutter
- making a return feel like starting over
- presenting continuity as a debt or backlog
- confusing a gap (continuity intact) with a loss (needs recovery — MOM-114)
- describing storage mechanics as if Momentum owned them (reference Knowledge Management)
