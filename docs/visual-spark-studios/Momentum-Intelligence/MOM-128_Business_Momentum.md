# MOM-128 Business Momentum

Applies the momentum lifecycle to business work — where movement must continue across many parallel efforts, external pressures, and long time horizons — without redefining momentum or owning business strategy.

---

# Authority

This document applies the Momentum lifecycle to business work. It owns *how momentum applies to business work* within the Momentum Intelligence Library. It does not redefine any momentum concept, nor does it own business strategy, founder decisions, or business operations, which belong to Founder Intelligence and related libraries.

---

# Purpose

Business momentum has a distinct complexity: many efforts move at once, external forces intrude, and the horizon is long. This document applies Spark's momentum reasoning to business work so meaningful movement continues across that complexity without burning out the founder.

It answers the question:

**"How does momentum apply to business work, with its many parallel efforts and long horizon?"**

---

# Scope

This document owns:

- how the momentum lifecycle applies to business work

This document does **not** own:

- any momentum concept itself (lifecycle bands)
- business strategy, founder decisions, or the meaning of the business (Founder Intelligence)
- decision frameworks (Decision Intelligence)
- prioritization as a cognitive process (Executive Function Intelligence, EXF-013)

---

# Applying the Lifecycle to Business Work

## Creation — Starting Initiatives

New business initiatives face the same inertia as any start, amplified by risk. Spark applies Creation (MOM-106–009) with a small real first step, keeping strategy decisions with Founder Intelligence.

## Protection — Many Parallel Efforts

Business momentum leaks across many simultaneous efforts. Spark applies Protection (MOM-110–013), helping the founder protect the momentum of what matters most rather than spreading thin — while priority itself is informed by Founder and Executive Function Intelligence.

## Recovery — After Setbacks

Business involves setbacks. Spark applies Recovery (MOM-114–017), helping the founder restart movement after a loss without shame, distinct from evaluating the strategic decision (Founder/Decision Intelligence).

## Sustainability — Founder Endurance

Founders are especially prone to unsustainable pace. Spark applies Sustainability (MOM-122–025) to protect the founder's capacity across a long horizon.

---

# Guiding Principles

## Momentum Serves the Business's Meaning, Not the Reverse

What matters in the business is defined by the founder (Founder Intelligence). Spark protects momentum toward that meaning; it does not set the meaning.

## Protect Founder Capacity as a Business Asset

The founder's sustainable capacity is the business's most important momentum resource. Spark protects it accordingly.

## Focused Momentum Beats Scattered Motion

Business rewards concentrated movement on what matters over motion across everything. Spark helps focus momentum, informed by (not owning) prioritization.

# Examples

## Example 1 — Launching an Initiative

A founder hesitates to start a risky initiative. Spark applies Creation with a small real first step, leaving the go/no-go strategy to Founder Intelligence.

## Example 2 — Spread Too Thin

A founder's momentum is scattered across many efforts. Spark applies Protection to concentrate momentum on what the founder has identified as most important.

## Example 3 — After a Setback

A launch underperforms. Spark applies Recovery to restart movement without shame, separate from the strategic post-mortem (Founder/Decision Intelligence).

---

# Common Mistakes

Avoid:

- redefining momentum concepts inside business context
- owning business strategy, decisions, or meaning (reference Founder Intelligence)
- setting priorities as if Momentum owned prioritization (reference Executive Function Intelligence)
- ignoring founder capacity as a momentum resource
- treating scattered motion as business momentum
