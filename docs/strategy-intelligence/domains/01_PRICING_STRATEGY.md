## Core principle

Price is one part of a larger value, positioning, customer-fit, delivery, and sustainability decision. Pricing Strategy must not assume that raising or lowering price is the answer. Maintaining the current price is a valid strategy when evidence for change is weak.

## Scope

Help members decide whether and how to:

- Raise, lower, or maintain price  
- Restructure packaging / tiers  
- Choose hourly, package, project, outcome, access, or subscription models  
- Move between monthly and annual  
- Price memberships, services, consulting, coaching, programs, retainers, packages, digital offers, workshops, events, productized services  
- Price new offers vs revise existing ones  
- Treat new vs existing customers differently  
- Grandfather, founding pricing, or staged transitions  
- Reduce discount dependence  
- Test before full implementation  

Does **not** assume higher price is always better. Distinguishes pricing from value, positioning, fit, awareness, conversion, retention, delivery, capacity, cash pressure, offer complexity, and discount dependence.

## Ownership / routing boundaries

Pricing Strategy owns the **strategic pricing decision**. After direction is clear, route (do not auto-route):

| Owner | Owns |
|-------|------|
| Pricing Strategy | Whether to change, structure, options, experiments, readiness, recommendation |
| Finance / Business Estate | Margin, cash-flow, forecasting, break-even, affordability detail |
| Marketing | Public pricing communication, positioning, campaign messaging |
| Create | Pricing pages, notices, proposals, scripts, offer documents |
| Projects | Coordinated rollout |
| Calendar | Transition, notice, and review dates |
| Board | High-impact or difficult-to-reverse choices |
| Talk It Out | Fear, guilt, identity conflict |

## Question families

Raise / lower / what to charge · underpriced · grandfather · premium tier · monthly vs annual · stop discounting · hourly vs package vs retainer · change price or reduce scope · raise before launch · new vs current customers · founding price · wrong audience · low conversion really price? · delivery sustainability · maintain current price.

## Hidden / underlying questions

Examples:

| Surface | Possible deeper questions |
|---------|---------------------------|
| People are not buying — lower the price? | Offer clear? Audience right? Value believable? Trust? Sales process? Price misaligned? Attracting price-sensitive prospects? Enough evidence? |
| Doing too much for what I charge | Over-scoped? Unclear boundaries? Inefficient delivery? Custom support? Simplify package? Raise? Move work elsewhere? Keep the offer? |
| Competitors charge less | Offers comparable? Customers comparable? Positioning different? Delivery model different? Their advantage or constraint? Relevant enough? |

## Evidence discipline

Differentiate: confirmed price · sales results · inquiry · conversion · retention · churn · delivery cost/time · outcomes · feedback · willingness-to-pay signals · competitor observations · founder fear · assumptions about affordability/churn/value.

Do **not** treat as proof: competitor prices · compliments as WTP · founder effort as customer value · one objection as market · one sale as demand · fear as predicted churn · low sales as “price too high.”

## Heuristics (guide reasoning)

Diagnose before changing price · price cannot repair unclear value alone · effort ≠ value · sustainability requires delivery support · new-customer changes easier to test · tiers only when differences are clear · discounts need reason/boundary/end · lower price can raise support burden · higher price may raise expectations · grandfathering protects trust but adds complexity · staged transition reduces risk but prolongs admin · support the intended business model · simplest structure that works · test when uncertainty high and reversibility available · maintain current price when evidence is weak.

## Option patterns

At most three meaningfully different initial options. Patterns include: maintain · raise new-only · raise all with notice · grandfather (full or limited) · premium tier · simpler lower tier · restructure package · reduce scope hold price · add value first · hourly→packages · packages→recurring · annual/monthly · narrow discounts · founding pilot · limited test · delay · retire unsustainable · reposition before price.

Reject cosmetic variants (Raise 10% / 15% / 20%). Prefer structural diversity.

## Trade-offs

Select only material dimensions: value alignment, outcome, positioning, trust, acquisition, conversion, retention, churn, revenue, margin, delivery capacity, support burden, complexity, accessibility, founder energy, predictability, cash timing, expectations, transition burden, flexibility, evidence quality.

## Risks & reversibility

Material risks only. Use canonical `Reversibility`: easily / moderately / difficult / unknown. New-prospect quotes and limited pilots are usually easier to reverse than broad existing-member raises or permanent grandfathering of a large base.

## Experiments

Blueprints include assumption, action, scope, duration/review, evidence, success signal, stop signal, next decision. Examples: new-customer price test · premium-tier test · scope hold-price test · annual-option test · discount-removal test · value interview. **Never** auto-create a Project.

## Success / stop / review

Success is not revenue alone — include fit, trust, capacity, clarity, margin, outcomes. Stop on confusion, promise/delivery mismatch, churn beyond threshold, support offsetting revenue, tier chaos, poor-fit attraction, weak evidence, expectations beyond capacity, trust damage without sustainability gain. Review when scope, demand, retention, capacity, outcomes, support, model, audience, evidence, experiment end, transition expiry, competitor shift, or repositioning changes.

## Adaptive Companion

Affects presentation only (Fewer Choices, One Step, Summary First, More Structure, Talk Freely, Examples). Never reduces reasoning quality.

## Quality rules

Reject guidance that assumes raise/lower always wins, uses only competitor pricing, equates effort with value, invents evidence, auto-recommends discounts, bypasses option readiness, uses arbitrary formulas as certainty, or marks a recommendation as confirmed.
