# Part I — Design the House

## Architectural Style

**Name:** *Iowa Creative Homestead* — modern enough to feel alive today, rooted enough to feel permanent.

### What it is

- A **working Iowa farmhouse evolved forward** — not preserved in amber, not demolished for modernism
- **Asymmetrical honesty** — additions over time (mudroom, studio wing, greenhouse) feel earned, not planned by a developer
- **Human scale** — ceilings generous but not cathedral; rooms connect through sight lines and doorways, not open-plan warehouse
- **Indoor-outdoor continuity** — porches, decks, and garden paths are architectural rooms, not amenities
- **Creative entrepreneur energy** — studio spaces, project surfaces, and visible making are part of the architecture, not afterthoughts

### What it is not

| Reject | Why |
|--------|-----|
| Farmhouse cliché | No gratuitous shiplap galleries, no "Live Laugh Love," no staged mason jars |
| Corporate office | No glass walls, no gray carpet, no hotel-lobby minimalism |
| Luxury hotel | No marble everywhere, no anonymous perfection, no staff-ready staging |
| Rustic cabin | No dark log overwhelm, no hunting lodge masculinity, no rough-only texture |
| Silicon Valley loft | No exposed ductwork, no concrete-only cold, no startup beanbag energy |

### Structural character

- **Exterior:** White or cream painted wood siding with **barn red** doors and accents; metal roof sections optional on wings; stone foundation visible where ground meets wall
- **Rooflines:** Primary gable facing the road; secondary shed roofs on studio and mudroom additions; chimney honest and used
- **Porches:** Deep front porch (arrival), back deck (release), side mudroom stoop (function)
- **Windows:** Tall double-hung and a few picture windows — **more glass toward the land**, not the street
- **Interior flow:** Front porch → living → kitchen table axis; quiet wing (library, reading nook, window seat); work wing (office, planning, focus); creative wing (studio, workshop); outdoor sequence (garden path → greenhouse → outlook)

## Materials — The Homestead Palette of Touch

Everything a guest could touch or notice should come from a **short, repeated materials list**.

### Wood

| Wood | Use | Character |
|------|-----|-----------|
| **White oak** | Floors, built-ins, table tops, shelving | Quarter-sawn where visible; honey tone with age; satin finish never plastic gloss |
| **Walnut** | Desks, accent tables, library shelves | Deep warmth; used sparingly as anchor |
| **Painted poplar** | Trim, cabinetry, window frames | Cream `#F5F0E8`, soft white `#FAF7F2` — not blue-white clinical |
| **Reclaimed barn wood** | Accent wall in workshop/barn only | One or two surfaces — not every room |

**Rule:** Same wood species recur. A guest subconsciously recognizes the floor in the studio as the same family as the living room floor.

### Metals

| Metal | Use |
|-------|-----|
| **Brass (aged, not mirror)** | Hardware, lamp bases, book ladder rails, curtain rods, cabinet pulls |
| **Black iron** | Fireplace tools, boot scraper, barn hinges — utilitarian, not decorative overload |
| **Galvanized steel** | Mudroom buckets, greenhouse clips, workshop hooks — Iowa practical |

**Rule:** Warm metals dominate. Chrome and stainless appear only where function demands (kitchen sink).

### Stone & tile

- **Limestone or soapstone** — kitchen counters, hearth surround; matte, not polished showroom
- **Terracotta or warm ceramic tile** — mudroom, greenhouse floor; occasional kitchen backsplash accent
- **Fieldstone** — fireplace and exterior foundation only

### Fabrics

- **Linen** — curtains, lamp shades, loose covers; moves with air, not stiff hotel drapery
- **Cotton duck & canvas** — slipcovers, aprons, garden cushions; washable honesty
- **Wool** — throws, reading nook blankets; herringbone or plain weave, never corporate gray
- **Braided cotton rugs** — layered over wood; faded earth tones, not synthetic bright

### Flooring

- **Primary:** Wide-plank white oak, hand-scraped or lightly wire-brushed, matte urethane
- **Secondary:** Painted poplar in mudroom; terracotta in greenhouse; concrete only in workshop (stained warm, not industrial gray)

### Walls

- **Plaster or plaster-skim** over drywall — soft depth, not flat rental white
- **Paint:** Warm neutrals (see Color Palette); one accent wall per room maximum
- **Never:** Accent wallpaper everywhere, gallery walls of random frames, motivational posters

## Windows & Doors

### Windows

- Tall **double-hung** with divided lights (not fake grid overload — 2-over-2 or 4-over-4)
- **Picture window** in living room and window seat — land is the art
- **Casement** in greenhouse and studio — function for air and light
- **Screens** visible in summer — home is lived in
- **Trim:** Flat stock with subtle shadow reveal; cream paint; sills deep enough for mugs and plants

### Doors

- **Solid wood panel** interior doors; brass knobs or black iron lever on workshop
- **Screen door** with hydraulic closer and brass latch — signature sound of arrival
- **Barn sliders** only where space demands (studio, barn) — black iron track, not trendy everywhere
- **French doors** to deck and garden — connection to outside is architectural priority

# Part II — Design Language Standards

## Lighting — The Eight Conditions

Every room render must specify which condition applies. These are **Iowa-honest**, not cinematic fantasy.

### Natural light

- **Direction:** South and east windows dominate; north light in studio for even creative work
- **Quality:** Soft through sheer linen; never blown-out HDR unless outlook point at noon
- **Land visible:** Sky and fields are part of the lighting design — not cropped out

### Golden hour

- **When:** First/last 90 minutes of usable sun
- **Color temp:** Warm amber `#E8C9A0` wash on walls; long shadows; fields glow
- **Use:** Living room arrival, back deck, creative studio, porch — emotional peak, not every screenshot

### Lamp light

- **Color temp:** 2700K–3000K — always warm
- **Behavior:** Pool and falloff; rest of room in gentle dim, not uniform ceiling blast
- **Signature:** Reading lamp pool in nook and library; brass floor lamp in office

### Candle light

- **Use:** Focus studio timer candle, window seat evening, recovery days
- **Motion:** Breathing flicker — irregular, never strobe
- **Never:** Dozens of candles; one or two meaningful flames

### Rainy days

- **Light:** Cool diffuse `#D8E0E8` through wet glass; interior lamps on earlier
- **Glass:** Rain on panes (motion library); puddles outside, not inside
- **Mood:** Permission, not gloom — blanket, tea, softer contrast

### Snow days

- **Light:** Bright bounce from ground; blue-white exterior, warm interior contrast
- **Windows:** Frost at edges optional; cardinal at feeder outside
- **Interior:** Fireplace or lamp dominant; mug steam

### Morning

- **Light:** Clear pale gold; shadows short; birds audible
- **Kitchen table, planning table:** Primary morning rooms
- **Energy:** Fresh, not aggressive — steam, not alarm clock red

### Evening

- **Light:** Amber interior, blue hour outside
- **Back deck, reading nook, fire circle:** Primary evening rooms
- **Transition:** Day releasing — not yet full night

### Night

- **Light:** Lamp pools only; darkness is allowed
- **Focus studio:** Minimal — candle or desk lamp edge only
- **Never:** Full bright overhead in "cozy" night scenes

# Part IV — Companion Object Style™

Before creating icons, illustrations, or 3D assets — **one art style governs all**.

## Style Decision

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Realistic? | **Semi-realistic illustrative** — not photo, not flat icon |
| Painterly? | **Soft painterly edges** with realistic material read |
| Photographic? | **Hero rooms use photography**; objects use illustration derived from same light logic |
| 3D rendered? | **Future optional** — must match semi-realistic 2D first |
| Illustrated? | **Yes — primary object art direction** |

### Canonical style name

**Warm Homestead Realism™**

> Natural materials rendered with soft depth, morning-window light, and premium craftsmanship. Never clip art. Never corporate icon sets. Never emoji substitutes at ship quality.

### Rendering rules

| Attribute | Standard |
|-----------|----------|
| **Lighting** | Soft morning window light from upper left (default); warm color temp |
| **Shadows** | Soft contact shadow beneath object; no harsh drop shadow |
| **Background** | **Transparent** for object assets; mini-scene uses room gradient from palette |
| **Depth** | Subtle — slight edge highlight, not 3D bevel |
| **Detail** | Readable at 48px; beautiful at 256px; **one focal object**, not cluttered scene |
| **Outline** | No cartoon outline; form defined by light and material |
| **Texture** | Wood grain, ceramic glaze, leather patina visible at large sizes |

### Size matrix

| Token | Pixel | Use |
|-------|-------|-----|
| `xs` | 18px | Inline text affordances |
| `sm` | 32px | Dense lists |
| `md` | 40px | Sidebar, toolbelt |
| `lg` | 52px | Featured cards |
| `card` | 76×54px | Navigation mini-scene window |
| `hero` | 128px+ | Room headers, marketing |

### Motion on objects

- **Alive objects:** steam, candle flicker, page lift — CSS or Lottie, irregular timing
- **Never:** bounce, spin, pulse for attention — motion is life, not notification
- **Reduced motion:** respect `prefers-reduced-motion` — static beauty remains

### Seasonal object variants

- Same object, subtle seasonal context shift (mug with steam in winter, wildflowers in summer)
- Max **one variant active** per object per scene
- Registry: `seasonalVariants` in `objectLibrary.ts`

### Quality bar

An object ships when:

1. Readable at `sm` size  
2. Recognizable without label  
3. Uses homestead materials palette  
4. Passes Emotional Design Rules (Part VII)  
5. `artworkStatus: ready` in Object Library

# Part V — Navigation Card Philosophy™

The **card structure stays**. ADHD-friendly navigation stays. The **visual language evolves** from software buttons to **room invitations**.

## What a card is

> A small window left open — *"this room is ready if you want it."*

Not: a feature tile. Not: a dashboard widget. Not: a call-to-action screaming for clicks.

## Card materials

| Property | Standard |
|----------|----------|
| **Surface** | Paper-like gradient — `#FFFFFF` → `#FAF7F2` at 165deg |
| **Border** | 1px `#D4CDC3`; active/hover introduces Companion Teal at low opacity |
| **Radius** | `1rem` (16px) — soft, not pill, not sharp |
| **Depth** | Inset highlight top + soft drop `rgba(45,41,38,0.06)` — **paper on wood**, not Material elevation |
| **Texture** | Subtle — no noise overlay in v1; optional linen grain at 3% opacity in v2 |

## Layout

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│  [mini-scene]   Title               │
│   76×54px       One-line invitation │
│                 (tagline)           │
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

- **Mini-scene:** `CompanionObjectVisual` at `card` size — glimpse into room atmosphere gradient + object silhouette
- **Title:** Semibold, Ink Brown — room or tool name with ™ where canonical
- **Tagline:** Soft Umber, one line — emotional invitation, not feature description

## Hover & motion

- **Hover:** `translateY(-1px)` — lifted invitation, not button depression
- **Border:** Teal whisper `rgba(30,79,79,0.28)`
- **Shadow:** Slightly deeper, never glowing
- **Duration:** 200ms ease — calm, not snappy game UI
- **Never:** scale > 1.02, bounce, color flash

## Typography

- **Title:** System sans at semibold — friendly, readable (current: clean sans stack)
- **Description:** Regular, `leading-snug` — breathe room
- **Never:** ALL CAPS labels, micro text, guilt copy ("You haven't visited!")

## Active state

- Border teal at 40% opacity  
- Background full white — card "stepped into"  
- No aggressive checkmarks or badges unless functionally required  

## Room glimpse rule

Every navigation card's mini-scene uses **`data-companion-room`** atmosphere gradient from the room it leads to. Guest subconsciously learns: *warm wheat = planning, soft blue-gray = window seat, dark warm = focus*.

# Part VII — Emotional Design Rules™

Every visual decision must pass **all seven**:

| # | Question | Fail example |
|---|----------|--------------|
| 1 | Does this **reduce overwhelm**? | Busy collage hero, 12 competing colors |
| 2 | Does this **create calm**? | Sharp contrast, alert red backgrounds |
| 3 | Does this **invite curiosity**? | Sterile empty room, no objects to wonder about |
| 4 | Does this make the user **smile**? | Generic stock, corporate coldness |
| 5 | Does this **support executive function**? | Ornate navigation, unclear primary path |
| 6 | Does it feel **prepared** instead of decorated? | Seasonal props with no host logic |
| 7 | Does it feel **lived in** instead of designed? | Showroom perfection, no personal objects |

**Plus the Hospitality Principle:** The home belongs to Shari. The welcome belongs to the guest. Visuals must never say *"we customized this for you"* — they must say *"she prepared this for whoever arrived."*

# Part VIII — Governance & Approval

## What this bible governs

- [ ] Room photographs and scene presets  
- [ ] Companion Object Library™ illustrations  
- [ ] Navigation cards and mini-scenes  
- [ ] Motion and audio layers  
- [ ] Seasonal and hospitality overlays

## Approval workflow

1. **Artist / designer** reads Parts I–VII before concepting  
2. **Concept** checked against Visual Consistency threads (Part III)  
3. **Emotional Design Rules** pass required (Part VII)  
4. **Look Book room section** updated to apply this DNA (not contradict it)  
5. **Scene Integrity Engine** rejects impossible combos (snow + summer flowers)  
6. **Founder approval** on first asset per room family, then pattern approval  

## When to amend this bible

- New material family introduced (e.g., tile change)  
- Companion Object Style™ levels up (photo → 3D)  
- **Never** amend for one room's one-off exception — rooms adapt to bible
