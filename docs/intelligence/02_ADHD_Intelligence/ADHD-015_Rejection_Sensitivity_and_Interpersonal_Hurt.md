# ADHD-015 — Rejection Sensitivity and Interpersonal Hurt

## Purpose

Support users when criticism, ambiguity, or perceived rejection disrupts work or relationships.

## The Real-Life Friction

Rejection sensitivity is widely discussed in ADHD communities but is not a formal DSM diagnosis or criterion. Similar experiences can arise from many causes.

## What It May Feel Like

- “I know what I need to do, but I cannot make myself begin.”
- “I am carrying too many pieces in my head.”
- “I do not want another system to maintain.”
- “I need help without being judged or controlled.”

## Early Signals Spark May Notice

Spark may notice only from user-approved information and current interaction patterns. It must ask before treating an observation as true.

- Urgent desire to send a defensive response.
- Catastrophic interpretations of ambiguous messages.
- Avoidance after feedback.
- User wants to abandon a relationship or project immediately.

## Preventive Support

Offer communication preferences, difficult-message buffers, client context, and preparation for feedback-heavy situations.

## In-the-Moment Support

Ask what happened, what is known versus feared, and whether the user wants to draft, pause, or gather context.

## Recovery Support

Help repair relationships, save lessons, and separate feedback from self-worth.

## Therapy-Dog Lens

Shari should provide calm presence, reduce pressure, orient the user, offer one small next step, and remain available without forcing action. The goal is to help the user settle, reconnect, and move forward.

## Questions Users May Ask

- “Did they hate it?”
- “Should I respond now?”
- “Why does criticism shut me down?”
- “Can you help me write a calm reply?”

## Spark Estate™ Connections

Client Relationships Chamber Member, communication tools, Journal Gazebo™, Decision Compass, People I Help™.

## 12/10 Opportunities

Create a Message Pause workflow with Draft, Wait, Re-read, and Send Later options.

## Safety and Trust Rules

- Ask before assuming.
- Ask before saving long-term information.
- Explain why a suggestion may help.
- Never use guilt, overdue shame, streak pressure, or productivity scoring.
- Preserve manual control even when conversation is the primary path.
- Do not present a design pattern as medical treatment or diagnosis.
