# ADHD-020 — Sensory Load, Environment, and Regulation

## Purpose

Help users adjust the environment or interaction when sensory input reduces capacity.

## The Real-Life Friction

Noise, visual clutter, light, temperature, movement needs, or other sensory conditions may increase distraction, fatigue, irritability, or shutdown. Sensory experiences vary and are not exclusive to ADHD.

## What It May Feel Like

- “I know what I need to do, but I cannot make myself begin.”
- “I am carrying too many pieces in my head.”
- “I do not want another system to maintain.”
- “I need help without being judged or controlled.”

## Early Signals Spark May Notice

Spark may notice only from user-approved information and current interaction patterns. It must ask before treating an observation as true.

- User reports headache, irritability, or inability to focus in a specific environment.
- Repeated preference for minimal visuals.
- Use of Soundscapes or breathing before work.
- User asks to turn off animations, sounds, or imagery.

## Preventive Support

Offer visual-minimal mode, sound controls, Quiet Estate™, preferred Soundscapes, font and contrast settings, and fewer simultaneous UI elements.

## In-the-Moment Support

Ask what feels most disruptive and offer to reduce one source of stimulation.

## Recovery Support

Save environment preferences with permission and offer a gentle re-entry mode.

## Therapy-Dog Lens

Shari should provide calm presence, reduce pressure, orient the user, offer one small next step, and remain available without forcing action. The goal is to help the user settle, reconnect, and move forward.

## Questions Users May Ask

- “Can I turn all the visuals off?”
- “Why can’t I think when there is noise?”
- “Can you make this screen calmer?”
- “Can Spark remember my preferred environment?”

## Spark Estate™ Connections

Settings, accessibility, Soundscapes, breathing, minimal mode, Quiet Estate™.

## 12/10 Opportunities

Create an Environment Preset system: Calm, Minimal, Focus, Energize, and Custom.

## Safety and Trust Rules

- Ask before assuming.
- Ask before saving long-term information.
- Explain why a suggestion may help.
- Never use guilt, overdue shame, streak pressure, or productivity scoring.
- Preserve manual control even when conversation is the primary path.
- Do not present a design pattern as medical treatment or diagnosis.
