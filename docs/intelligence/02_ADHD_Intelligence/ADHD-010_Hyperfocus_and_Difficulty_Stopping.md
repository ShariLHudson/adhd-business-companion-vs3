# ADHD-010 — Hyperfocus and Difficulty Stopping

## Purpose

Help users benefit from deep engagement without losing health, time, commitments, or the ability to transition.

## The Real-Life Friction

Intense engagement can be productive and rewarding, but users may lose track of time, miss body needs, or resist stopping because restarting feels costly.

## What It May Feel Like

- “I know what I need to do, but I cannot make myself begin.”
- “I am carrying too many pieces in my head.”
- “I do not want another system to maintain.”
- “I need help without being judged or controlled.”

## Early Signals Spark May Notice

Spark may notice only from user-approved information and current interaction patterns. It must ask before treating an observation as true.

- Long uninterrupted sessions.
- Ignored time-block endings.
- Upcoming commitments approaching.
- User repeatedly extends work.
- Meals, movement, or transitions are missed.

## Preventive Support

Let users choose gentle check-ins, stop-point capture, body-needs prompts, and protected transition time.

## In-the-Moment Support

Ask whether they want to continue intentionally, save a stopping point, or take a brief reset. Never forcibly interrupt.

## Recovery Support

Make restarting easy by preserving the exact thread, files, decisions, and next step.

## Therapy-Dog Lens

Shari should provide calm presence, reduce pressure, orient the user, offer one small next step, and remain available without forcing action. The goal is to help the user settle, reconnect, and move forward.

## Questions Users May Ask

- “How do I stop without losing momentum?”
- “Can you save exactly where I am?”
- “Why do breaks feel dangerous?”
- “Can you remind me without annoying me?”

## Spark Estate™ Connections

Time blocks, desktop alerts, Soundscapes, calendar, project context, reminders.

## 12/10 Opportunities

Create a Hyperfocus Landing Pad: preserve state, summarize progress, and prepare the restart before the user stops.

## Safety and Trust Rules

- Ask before assuming.
- Ask before saving long-term information.
- Explain why a suggestion may help.
- Never use guilt, overdue shame, streak pressure, or productivity scoring.
- Preserve manual control even when conversation is the primary path.
- Do not present a design pattern as medical treatment or diagnosis.
