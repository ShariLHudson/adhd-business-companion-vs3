# ADHD-023 — Impulsivity, Risk, and Fast Decisions

## Purpose

Support thoughtful action without suppressing creativity, spontaneity, or entrepreneurial courage.

## The Real-Life Friction

Fast decisions may involve spending, commitments, messages, offers, tool changes, or new projects. Some are valuable; others create avoidable burden.

## What It May Feel Like

- “I know what I need to do, but I cannot make myself begin.”
- “I am carrying too many pieces in my head.”
- “I do not want another system to maintain.”
- “I need help without being judged or controlled.”

## Early Signals Spark May Notice

Spark may notice only from user-approved information and current interaction patterns. It must ask before treating an observation as true.

- Sudden purchase or launch plans.
- New tool replacing a recently adopted tool.
- High-consequence decision made during strong emotion.
- Many new commitments added quickly.

## Preventive Support

Offer optional cooling-off windows, decision criteria, budget boundaries, and pre-mortems for high-impact choices.

## In-the-Moment Support

Ask whether the user wants a quick decision, a risk check, or time to reconsider. Do not block ordinary low-risk choices.

## Recovery Support

Review outcomes without blame and update decision guardrails with permission.

## Therapy-Dog Lens

Shari should provide calm presence, reduce pressure, orient the user, offer one small next step, and remain available without forcing action. The goal is to help the user settle, reconnect, and move forward.

## Questions Users May Ask

- “Should I buy this?”
- “Am I chasing another tool?”
- “Is this a good opportunity or a distraction?”
- “Can you help me slow this down without killing the idea?”

## Spark Estate™ Connections

Decision Compass, Business Lens, values, Finance Chamber, calendar, project portfolio.

## 12/10 Opportunities

Create a Consequence-Aware Pause for decisions involving money, public commitments, or major changes.

## Safety and Trust Rules

- Ask before assuming.
- Ask before saving long-term information.
- Explain why a suggestion may help.
- Never use guilt, overdue shame, streak pressure, or productivity scoring.
- Preserve manual control even when conversation is the primary path.
- Do not present a design pattern as medical treatment or diagnosis.
