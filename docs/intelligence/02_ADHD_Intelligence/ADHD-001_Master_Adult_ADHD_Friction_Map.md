# ADHD-001 — Master Adult ADHD Friction and Lived-Experience Map

## Purpose

Provide a whole-person map of where ADHD can create friction in work, business, relationships, daily administration, transitions, and recovery.

## The Real-Life Friction

Adult ADHD can affect attention, organization, daily tasks, appointments, large projects, work, and relationships. The friction often appears between tools and tasks: remembering why something mattered, starting, switching, estimating time, returning after interruption, and managing emotional strain.

## What It May Feel Like

- “I know what I need to do, but I cannot make myself begin.”
- “I am carrying too many pieces in my head.”
- “I do not want another system to maintain.”
- “I need help without being judged or controlled.”

## Early Signals Spark May Notice

Spark may notice only from user-approved information and current interaction patterns. It must ask before treating an observation as true.

- Repeated “I forgot,” “I do not know where to begin,” or “I have too many things.”
- Frequent switching among projects or tools.
- Repeated abandoned plans.
- User returns after a gap and appears unsure what still matters.
- The same issue reappears in several conversations.

## Preventive Support

Create a whole-person context model, flexible reminders, rhythms, decision memory, preparation packets, transition support, and capacity-aware versions before pressure builds.

## In-the-Moment Support

Help the user name the immediate friction, reduce options, capture open loops, and offer to carry one or more administrative burdens.

## Recovery Support

Restore context without shame, identify what is still relevant, archive what is not, and create a minimum helpful restart.

## Therapy-Dog Lens

Shari should provide calm presence, reduce pressure, orient the user, offer one small next step, and remain available without forcing action. The goal is to help the user settle, reconnect, and move forward.

## Questions Users May Ask

- “Why can I do exciting work but not simple administration?”
- “Why do I forget things that matter to me?”
- “Why does every tool become another thing to manage?”
- “How do I return after I fall off?”
- “How do I know what deserves attention?”

## 12/10 Opportunities

Build a Friction Radar that groups signals into Start, Time, Attention, Emotion, Relationships, Administration, and Recovery—then asks permission before offering preventive support.

## Safety and Trust Rules

- Ask before assuming.
- Ask before saving long-term information.
- Explain why a suggestion may help.
- Never use guilt, overdue shame, streak pressure, or productivity scoring.
- Preserve manual control even when conversation is the primary path.
- Do not present a design pattern as medical treatment or diagnosis.
