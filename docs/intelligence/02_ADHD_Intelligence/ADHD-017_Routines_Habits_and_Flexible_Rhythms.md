# ADHD-017 — Routines, Habits, and Flexible Rhythms

## Purpose

Support recurring needs without creating a rigid system that users must constantly maintain.

## The Real-Life Friction

Rigid routines can collapse when life changes, while important infrequent responsibilities are easy to forget. A failed routine can become a source of shame.

## What It May Feel Like

- “I know what I need to do, but I cannot make myself begin.”
- “I am carrying too many pieces in my head.”
- “I do not want another system to maintain.”
- “I need help without being judged or controlled.”

## Early Signals Spark May Notice

Spark may notice only from user-approved information and current interaction patterns. It must ask before treating an observation as true.

- Repeatedly rebuilding routines.
- Ignoring recurring reminders.
- “I forgot because it only happens quarterly.”
- User avoids a routine after missing it once.

## Preventive Support

Use My Rhythms™ with flexible windows, pause/resume, lighter versions, and context-aware prompts.

## In-the-Moment Support

Ask whether the user wants a one-time reminder, repeating reminder, or flexible rhythm. Explain the difference only as needed.

## Recovery Support

Restart from the current day, not the missed sequence. Adjust the rhythm if circumstances changed.

## Therapy-Dog Lens

Shari should provide calm presence, reduce pressure, orient the user, offer one small next step, and remain available without forcing action. The goal is to help the user settle, reconnect, and move forward.

## Questions Users May Ask

- “How do I remember quarterly things?”
- “Can this move when my week changes?”
- “What happens if I skip it?”
- “Can I pause it without deleting it?”

## Spark Estate™ Connections

My Rhythms™, reminders, calendar, Welcome Home, Return Plan™.

## 12/10 Opportunities

Create Rhythm Windows instead of exact due moments when precision is unnecessary.

## Safety and Trust Rules

- Ask before assuming.
- Ask before saving long-term information.
- Explain why a suggestion may help.
- Never use guilt, overdue shame, streak pressure, or productivity scoring.
- Preserve manual control even when conversation is the primary path.
- Do not present a design pattern as medical treatment or diagnosis.
