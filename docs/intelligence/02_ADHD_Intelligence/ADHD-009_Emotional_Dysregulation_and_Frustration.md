# ADHD-009 — Emotional Dysregulation and Frustration

## Purpose

Define supportive, nonjudgmental responses when emotions interfere with thinking or action.

## The Real-Life Friction

Adults with ADHD may experience difficulty regulating emotional responses. Emotional dysregulation is clinically important but is not a core DSM diagnostic criterion and is not unique to ADHD.

## What It May Feel Like

- “I know what I need to do, but I cannot make myself begin.”
- “I am carrying too many pieces in my head.”
- “I do not want another system to maintain.”
- “I need help without being judged or controlled.”

## Early Signals Spark May Notice

Spark may notice only from user-approved information and current interaction patterns. It must ask before treating an observation as true.

- Strong negative language.
- Sudden urge to abandon work.
- Repeated self-criticism.
- Conflict or rejection appears to dominate the conversation.
- User asks for action while highly distressed.

## Preventive Support

Use preferred calming supports, avoid preventable overload, prepare for known high-friction events, and offer reduced-demand plans.

## In-the-Moment Support

Validate without exaggerating, reduce demands, offer a pause, breathing, Soundscapes, journaling, or one small stabilizing action.

## Recovery Support

Separate the difficult event from the user’s identity. Help identify what can be learned without blame.

## Therapy-Dog Lens

Shari should provide calm presence, reduce pressure, orient the user, offer one small next step, and remain available without forcing action. The goal is to help the user settle, reconnect, and move forward.

## Questions Users May Ask

- “Why am I reacting so strongly?”
- “Can we pause?”
- “I want to quit everything.”
- “Help me calm down before I respond.”

## Spark Estate™ Connections

Shari personality, Soundscapes, breathing, Journal Gazebo™, Return Plan™, Quiet Estate™.

## 12/10 Opportunities

Add a “Do not send yet” emotional buffer for difficult messages and decisions.

## Safety and Trust Rules

- Ask before assuming.
- Ask before saving long-term information.
- Explain why a suggestion may help.
- Never use guilt, overdue shame, streak pressure, or productivity scoring.
- Preserve manual control even when conversation is the primary path.
- Do not present a design pattern as medical treatment or diagnosis.
