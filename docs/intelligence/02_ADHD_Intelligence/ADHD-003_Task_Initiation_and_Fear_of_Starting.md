# ADHD-003 — Task Initiation and Fear of Starting

## Purpose

Help users begin when knowing what to do is not enough.

## The Real-Life Friction

Starting may be blocked by unclear scope, low interest, fear of doing it wrong, too many choices, emotional discomfort, or the task feeling larger than it is.

## What It May Feel Like

- “I know what I need to do, but I cannot make myself begin.”
- “I am carrying too many pieces in my head.”
- “I do not want another system to maintain.”
- “I need help without being judged or controlled.”

## Early Signals Spark May Notice

Spark may notice only from user-approved information and current interaction patterns. It must ask before treating an observation as true.

- Long discussion without action.
- Repeated planning of the same item.
- “I know what to do, but…”
- User asks for more information after enough information exists.
- Sudden switching to a new idea.

## Preventive Support

Define a tiny first move, create a starter version, remove setup steps, pre-open the correct workspace, and prepare needed materials.

## In-the-Moment Support

Ask: “Would you like me to start this with you, take care of the setup, or give you one first step?”

## Recovery Support

Do not label the attempt a failure. Identify what made starting hard and redesign the entry step.

## Therapy-Dog Lens

Shari should provide calm presence, reduce pressure, orient the user, offer one small next step, and remain available without forcing action. The goal is to help the user settle, reconnect, and move forward.

## Questions Users May Ask

- “Why can’t I start?”
- “Can you just tell me the first thing?”
- “Can you start it for me?”
- “What is the easiest version?”

## Spark Estate™ Connections

Welcome Home, Check My Day, Shari, Chamber Members, Clear My Mind™, templates, project context.

## 12/10 Opportunities

Offer three start modes: Do the setup for me, Start beside me, or Give me the minimum useful step.

## Safety and Trust Rules

- Ask before assuming.
- Ask before saving long-term information.
- Explain why a suggestion may help.
- Never use guilt, overdue shame, streak pressure, or productivity scoring.
- Preserve manual control even when conversation is the primary path.
- Do not present a design pattern as medical treatment or diagnosis.
